# Math for Machine Learning and AI — a quick guide

---
**Warning: this content is available only in PL language!!!**

This is not an academic textbook. It is a short book that builds **intuition through examples** and helps you understand *what AI is actually doing with numbers*.

If you have ever felt that:

- “probability and statistics seem simple in theory, but in practice something never quite adds up”,
- “embeddings and attention sound like magic”,
- “models sound smart, but it is unclear where the decisions come from”,

then this guide is here to help you make sense of it.

## What you get

- **PDF** guide
- approach: **example → fix your mental model → minimal math**
- lots of examples (from everyday life and from AI)

## Who it is for

- people getting into ML/AI (students, analysts, PMs, engineers, data scientists)
- those who want to understand LLMs without a “black box”
- those who prefer **concrete ideas and mental models** over formal definitions

You do not need advanced math. Formulas appear only where they genuinely help.

## What is inside

- **Probability**: decisions under uncertainty, thresholds, cost of errors, what “80% confidence” means in practice.
- **Statistics**: when a result is “stable” vs luck, how to think about data without fooling yourself.
- **Linear algebra**: why models “think in vectors”, what similarity means (geometry), and why matrices are the heart of neural networks.
- **Embeddings & (self-)attention**: tokenization, embeddings as “meaning in space”, self-attention as mixing information between tokens, softmax without magic, multi-head, and what happens during generation.

The attention chapter also includes worked mini-examples step by step (where the numbers come from, how weights are formed, and how vectors get mixed).

## The main takeaway

> AI does not “understand the world” the way humans do.  
> It describes the world with numbers, learns weights, and makes decisions under uncertainty.

## What comes after this book

After reading it, you will have a solid foundation to move into:

- practical ML (classification, metrics, validation, probability calibration)
- NLP/LLMs (RAG, embeddings, context limits, why models hallucinate)
- recommendations and ranking (user–item embeddings, similarity, cold start)
- risk and decisions (thresholds, policies, cost of errors, “when not to decide”)
- A/B tests and experiments (how to measure change without fooling yourself)
